# 10. Presence, discovery & trust zones (unified identity-assurance model)

Date: 2026-07-08

## Status

Proposed

## Context

Burble has, in pieces, most of what a rich presence system needs — but no
document ties them into one coherent model:

* **ADR-0002** — optional IndieWeb-native identity (IndieAuth, `rel=me`,
  h-card discovery). Your domain *is* your identity; the `rel=me` graph is
  effectively a social identity graph.
* **ADR-0003** — tiered caller authentication (PAKE/SAS) with a Tier-1
  identity key published via NAPTR/DNSSEC.
* **`Burble.Bolt.Spa`** — HMAC Single-Packet-Authorisation on the Bolt poke
  (authenticate-before-reachable at the IP layer).
* **`Burble.Security.SDP`** — a Zero-Trust Software-Defined Perimeter that
  *also* uses SPA + dynamic firewalling (authenticate-before-visible at the
  network layer).
* **BLE-SPA** (design, `docs/architecture/ANDROID-CLIENT.adoc`) — the same
  knock, at the Bluetooth advertising layer.

Two gaps remain. First, **discovery is described per-transport** (BLE in the
Android doc, relay/rooms in the web client) with no shared notion of
"who may find me, here." Second, **identity-verification strength and
who-can-reach-me are never connected** — ADR-0003 tiers auth, ADR-0002 links
identity, but nothing says "a stronger-verified contact may occupy a more
trusted position."

This ADR unifies them: **presence is a transport-agnostic policy layer;
trust zones decide who may discover/reach you; and the strength of a
contact's identity verification decides which zone they may occupy.** It does
not replace ADR-0002/0003 — it is the umbrella over them.

## Decision

### 1. Presence is transport-agnostic; it is rendezvous, not the call

"Who is around and may I reach them" is one concept regardless of the pipe:

[cols="1,2,2"]
|===
| Transport | "Around" means | Shines for

| BLE (proximity) | in physical radio range | in-person: party, street
| Local network (mDNS/DNS-SD) | on the same wifi/subnet | lecture theatre, office, café
| Internet (relay / rooms) | anyone you've connected with | remote friends, corporate
|===

The presence layer answers discovery + *may-I-reach-you*, then **hands off to
the existing transports** (WebRTC per ADR-0003, Bolt per ADR-0004) for the
actual session. Presence never becomes a second media transport.

### 2. Trust zones are the policy layer (IE/Windows-zones model)

Borrowed from the Internet Explorer / Windows security-zones idea, applied to
presence:

[cols="1,2,2"]
|===
| Zone | Default posture | Feel

| **Public** (untrusted by nature) | Silent — broadcast nothing; rendezvous only on an authenticated knock | a bar, a stranger's party
| **Private** (known context) | Continuous contact-resolvable beacon | home wifi, your desk
| **Trusted-by-nature** | Presence to a verified group | a venue/event that attests its attendees; a family group
| **Trusted-by-you** ("make-me-trusted") | Explicit grant to named people/devices | "these people can always find me"
|===

Membership criteria for each zone MUST be explicit and auditable. In
particular *Trusted-by-nature* is the fuzzy one — learn from IE's "Local
Intranet" zone (a persistent security hole because "what counts" was vague):
its membership must be a concrete, forgery-resistant attestation (a signed
venue/event key, a verified group), never an implicit heuristic.

### 3. Modes are a cadence of the same crypto (not two systems)

* **Silent** = broadcast nothing; a one-shot HMAC **knock** to rendezvous
  (BLE-SPA / `Bolt.Spa`).
* **Presence** = continuously advertise a **rotating, contact-resolvable
  beacon** (the Find-My pattern: address rotated from a shared key so
  contacts resolve "that's Alice" while bystanders see unlinkable random
  bytes every ~15 min).
* **Off** = no activity. **This is the default**, so an unaware user is
  public-safe.

"Home presence" therefore is *not* a plaintext "I'm here" to the whole
street — it is continuous *contact-scoped* visibility. Same secret, two duty
cycles.

### 4. Resolution precedence: block > allow > zone (block is cryptographic)

Per contact, resolve in this order:

  block-list  →  allow-list  →  zone default

* **Block is absolute and zone-independent.** A blocked party can never
  resolve or reach you — not in a "trusted" venue, not at any claimed trust
  level. **Block is enforced cryptographically, not cosmetically:** revoking
  a contact = **rotate the presence secret and re-share to everyone except
  them**. Hiding them in the UI while they still hold the key is not blocking.
  This is the invariant that makes the safety case (below) real.
* **Allow overrides zone-down.** In Public/Silent, an allow-listed contact
  can still find you (the "invisible to the room except my friend" case).

### 5. Identity-verification ladder → assurance → zone eligibility

The verification *method* yields an *assurance level*, and assurance is the
input to which zone a contact may occupy. The system **grows with you**:
start where it's easiest, and each anchor you add raises your assurance and
widens your verified graph.

[cols="1,2,2,2"]
|===
| Rung | Method | Trust anchor | Notes

| In-person | QR scan (WeChat-style) | physical presence | strongest, zero infrastructure
| Web (easy) | `.well-known/burble` proof / `rel=me` / **IndieAuth** (ADR-0002) | your TLS cert (the MTA-STS pattern — HTTPS/WebPKI, *no DNSSEC*) | the accessible tier; **this is the IndieWeb social graph, elevated**
| Crypto | **IPNS / IPFS** | self-certifying public key | strong, decentralised, no DNSSEC or CA
| DNS | DANE (`OPENPGPKEY` / `TLSA`) | DNSSEC | for the DNS-savvy; DNSSEC is the real barrier
| Org | **NAPTR/DNSSEC** (ADR-0003 Tier 1) | corporate DNS | the corporate/enterprise top rung
|===

**Burble elevates the IndieWeb `rel=me` graph.** IndieWeb already *has* the
cute social graph; what it lacks is cryptographic meaning. Burble supplies the
signed identity proof on top, turning "fun social linking" into *verified
social identity*. A tiny bridge plugin (or a static file) emits the proof;
no WordPress theme, plugin swarm, or dangerous settings — see ADR-0002.

### 6. Cryptographic notes (so nobody re-treads these)

* **DNSSEC *signing* algorithm** (protecting the record) must be
  ecosystem-supported: **Ed25519 (algo 15)** or **ECDSA P-256/P-384 (13/14)**.
  **Ed448 (algo 16) is specified but not practically usable** — thin
  registrar/resolver support — so it must NOT be the zone-signing algorithm.
* **The identity key *inside* a record is opaque to DNS.** It may therefore be
  **Ed448, or an Ed448 + Kyber1024 (ML-KEM) hybrid**, published as the payload
  of a record that is DNSSEC-signed with Ed25519/P-256. Ed448/PQ was never off
  the table for the *identity* key — only as the *DNSSEC* algorithm. Note:
  Kyber in OpenPGP is still draft, so a PQ-hybrid identity is a Burble-native
  format until PQ-OpenPGP standardises.
* **Two anchor families, two rungs:** WebPKI/`.well-known` (MTA-STS pattern —
  accessible, trusts CAs) and DNSSEC/DANE (stronger anchor, needs DNSSEC).
* `SMIMEA` needs an (often paid) S/MIME cert; `SSHFP` is for SSH host keys
  (wrong use); **`APL` is experimental and effectively dead — do not use it.**
* **"Basic protection" is checked via device-lock, not SIM-PIN.** A SIM is a
  low-assurance possession factor (SIM-swap), and SIM-PIN status is not
  reliably queryable via public phone APIs. `KeyguardManager.isDeviceSecure()`
  (device has a PIN/pattern/biometric) *is* queryable and is the better
  signal; gate the basic tier on that.

### 7. Safety, duress & duty of care

The system must serve the person who needs to reach a trusted friend from an
unsafe situation without anyone noticing — *and* must not become a stalking
vector:

* **Covert must be genuinely covert.** A duress knock produces no screen
  flash, sound, notification, or BLE pattern a bystander could detect as
  signalling. If it has a tell, it is worse than nothing.
* **"Ghost — appear offline to everyone, now" overrides even the allow-list**,
  and is always one gesture away. It is both a privacy control and a safety
  control (escaping someone who is on, or was coerced onto, your allow-list).
* **It complements, never replaces, emergency services.** BLE/local range and
  "friend must be attending" mean this is "discreetly reach a nearby friend,"
  never a panic button. Over-promising a safety feature is dangerous; frame it
  honestly.
* Block-list (§4, cryptographic) is the backbone of all of the above.

## Relationship to existing decisions

* **ADR-0002** — the IndieWeb integration *is* the "Web (easy)" identity rung
  and the social-graph substrate this ADR elevates. Unchanged; referenced.
* **ADR-0003** — PAKE/SAS is the *pairing* mechanism; its NAPTR/DNSSEC Tier 1
  is the "Org" rung here. This ADR should be captured as, or alongside, the
  ADR-0003 identity-layer extension.
* **ADR-0004 / `Bolt.Spa` / `Security.SDP`** — the "authenticate-before-
  visible" SPA primitive appears at three layers (network SDP, IP Bolt, BLE);
  a future note may unify them as one primitive.

## Deferred / open questions

* **Route-privacy layer** (proxy, jump servers, oblivious DNS, VPN egress, and
  `Burble.Security.SDP`) is a *separate* layer beneath presence. It is **not
  designed here** — but the seam is reserved: each zone carries a (currently
  unpopulated) **route-policy** field `direct | relay | jump | odns |
  vpn-egress`. `Security.SDP` is the network-layer twin of the knock and the
  natural first inhabitant. Its own ADR when built.
* **Key bootstrap details** — the precise QR / `.well-known` / IndieAuth proof
  format and the secret-exchange handshake.
* **Coerced consent** — mitigations beyond the ghost override.
* **PQ-OpenPGP dependency** — Kyber-in-record rides a draft standard.

## Consequences

### Positive
* One coherent model spanning BLE, local-network, and internet presence.
* Verification strength gates trust position — principled, and it *grows with
  the user* (adoptability engine).
* Elevates the IndieWeb graph into verified social identity; honours the P2P
  and IndieWeb ethos; corporate NAPTR path keeps it enterprise-adoptable.
* A genuinely protective (and honestly-scoped) duress capability.

### Negative
* Large design surface; must be built incrementally behind flags.
* Cryptographic block (key rotation on revoke) is more work than a UI hide —
  but it is the safety-critical correct behaviour.
* Zone membership (esp. Trusted-by-nature) needs careful, forgery-resistant
  definition or it becomes the soft underbelly.

### Neutral
* Additive; existing auth, rooms, and transports are unchanged.
* Nothing here is built yet — this ADR pins the design before code so the
  simple default and the deferred seams are both explicit.
